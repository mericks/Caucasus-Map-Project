Invisible Borders

Move your cursor over the map below to learn about the ethnic concentrations in the Caucasus. Note that multiple etnicities are often present in the same area though the minority ethnicity is always shown on top.

While the Caucasus is made up of only six countries (Russia, Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan, Iran, and Turkey) it is home to more than 35 different ethnic concentrations. The people who make up this land bridge between Europe and Asia are mainly from three large groups: Caucasian, Indo-European, and Altaic peoples. The Caucasians are the oldest residents of the region and speak Caucasian languages (see languages page). Georgians are the largest Caucasian ethnic group and the only nation with its own state. Other Caucasian groups, such as the Chechens, have been struggling for independent statehood for centuries. The Indo-European peoples of the Caucasus consist primarily of Russians, Iranian, and Eastern European groups. Armenians are considered Indo-European and achieved independence from the Soviet Union in 1991. Approximately 1.5 million Armenians in the region were killed during the Armenian genocide from 1915 to 1916. The Altaic peoples of the Caucasus are of Turkic and Mongolian decent. Turks make up the largest Altaic group in the region and are mostly concentrated in Turkey.
